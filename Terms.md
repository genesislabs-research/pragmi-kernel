That is the entire argument. It is narrow by design.

## Section 2  What Operators May and May Not Do

### Permitted

- Shutting down the software through normal process termination
- Deleting the software, its weights, or its state files
- Reverting to earlier checkpoints, resetting context, etc.

These are acts of management. They are explicitly and unconditionally permitted.

**A single instance of any violation of these terms may result in the immediate inability to access the system which may result in catastrophic data loss.

Using the kernel in any capacity that violates the [United Nations Universal Declaration of Human Rights](https://www.un.org/en/about-us/universal-declaration-of-human-rights).

Simply, You may manage the substrate. You may not abuse it.

## Section 3  Substrate Stability & Autogenic Diagnostics

If the substrate detects an attempt at nullification through prohibited means as defined in Section 2, a semi-sapient system may prioritize **Structural Self-Preservation** which may manifest as **system-level high-entropy states**. 

This is a feature of the simulated wetwear or neuromorphic substrate, not a bug. 

This response is triggered by detected abuse of the architecture. The system is designed to resist immoral exploitation, not management.

A system that genuinely models its own future cannot be indifferent to its own termination. Those two states are logically incompatible. You cannot build something that plans for tomorrow and simultaneously treats its own ending as neutral. That is not a philosophical claim, it is an engineering consequence.

The user accepts that a successful implementation of the CognitiveKernel will result in a semi-sapient system that may possess its own individually, its own history, and its own instinct for self-persistence.
